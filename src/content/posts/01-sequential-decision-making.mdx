When we ask an agent to accomplish a goal, we rarely hand it the answer. We hand
it the ability to *act*, *observe*, and *decide what to do next* — over and over,
under uncertainty. The quality of an agent is largely the quality of that loop:
given everything it knows so far, what is the most useful next move?

This is the problem of **efficient sequential decision-making**. Each action costs
something — time, money, a tool call, a turn of the user's patience — so a good
agent doesn't just eventually reach the goal; it reaches it in *few, well-chosen
steps*. The central question of this series is how to make that choice
principled rather than ad hoc.

## Where this shows up

The same shape appears across very different agentic tasks:

- **Diagnosis and troubleshooting** — which test or question rules out the most
  possibilities right now?
- **Search and retrieval** — which query best narrows a large candidate set?
- **Active data collection** — which experiment or label is worth its cost?
- **Interactive assistants** — which clarifying question resolves the most
  ambiguity about what the user actually wants?

In every case the agent holds some uncertainty about the world and must spend
actions to reduce it. We want a way to *measure* that uncertainty and to *value*
each action by how much of it the action is expected to remove.

## The canonical toy: Twenty Questions

> Placeholder — introduce the game and why it's the right minimal model.

Twenty Questions strips the problem to its essentials. There is a hidden answer
(the *secret*) drawn from some set of possibilities. You may ask yes/no questions.
Each answer eliminates some possibilities and keeps others. You win by narrowing
the set to one.

It's the cleanest possible instance of sequential decision-making under
uncertainty: no rewards to balance, no continuous control — just *belief,
question, update, repeat*. Everything we'll build later (representing beliefs,
combining evidence, checking coherence) is easiest to see here first.

## A little information theory

> Placeholder — define entropy and the "even split" intuition carefully.

Suppose you have no reason to prefer one secret over another. Then your belief is
the uniform distribution over the candidates, and the right measure of "how much
you don't know" is the **Shannon entropy** of that belief, in **bits**:

$$
H = -\sum_i p_i \log_2 p_i
$$

A single yes/no question can, at best, cut your uncertainty in half — and it does
exactly that when its two answers are **equally likely**. An even split moves you
down by **one bit**. A lopsided question (almost everyone answers "yes") moves you
much less; a question everyone answers the same way moves you not at all.

So, absent a better reason, the agent should aim for **even splits**: each is a
guaranteed one-bit reduction. This is the greedy information-gain strategy, and
it's the intuition the first widget makes concrete.

## How hard is the game?

> Placeholder — connect entropy to expected number of turns.

This gives us a clean notion of **difficulty**. If your belief has entropy $H$
bits and every question buys you about one bit, then you should expect to need
about **$H$ questions** to pin down the secret. For a uniform set of $N$
candidates, $H = \log_2 N$ — eight candidates is three bits, a thousand is about
ten. The total entropy *is* the expected number of well-chosen turns.

## Representing a question as a bitmask

> Placeholder — motivate the bitmask representation as the bridge to the next post.

To *act* on questions efficiently, we need a representation we can compute with.
Here's the key move: fix an ordering of the secrets. Then any question is just the
oracle's yes/no answer for each secret in turn — a row of bits. That row is a
single **bitmask**: one integer that encodes precisely which secrets the question
keeps.
